## J1 — School Enables Fee Top-Up Credit

**Actor**: Headteacher / School Admin
**Mental state entering**: Interested but wary. Enabling a credit product is heavier than enabling an instalment plan — the admin is committing the school's paybill as a repayment routing channel and implicitly endorsing a credit offer going to their parents. The first question in their head is: *"If a parent can't repay, does the school owe money?"* Trust must be established — specifically, clarity that the school bears zero financial risk — before the admin will proceed.

### Trigger
Admin navigates to Settings → Fee Collection → Fee Top-Up Credit, either proactively (after having seen results from Layer 1) or following a prompt from a Zeraki customer success contact.

> **Dependency gate**: Layer 1 (Instalment Plans) must be enabled before Layer 2 can be activated. If the admin arrives at this screen without Layer 1 active, the system must explain the dependency clearly — not just block with "Instalment Plans must be enabled first." The message should orient the admin: *"Fee Top-Up Credit requires Instalment Plans to be active because it uses plan adherence data to assess eligibility for some parents. Enable Instalment Plans first, then return here."* The CTA should link directly to the Layer 1 settings screen.

### Journey Steps

**1. Landing on the settings screen**
Admin sees the product description. The framing carries enormous weight: this is a *lending product* routed through the school's paybill. The admin's immediate concern — "does the school owe money if a parent defaults?" — must be answered before the commitment statement is displayed.

The description should surface three things explicitly:
- Eligibility is determined by Zeraki's payment history analysis — the school does not select or recommend anyone
- The lending partner provides the capital and holds all default risk
- The school's role is the routing channel only — incoming fee payments are split, not generated from school funds

> **Friction point — credit product misread**: Admins may conflate "the school's paybill is used for repayment routing" with "the school is acting as a lender." A prominent one-line callout before the commitment statement removes this confusion: *"The school takes no financial risk. The lending partner provides the credit and holds the default risk."*

**2. Reading the commitment statement**
*"By enabling Fee Top-Up Credit, this school agrees that its M-Pesa paybill will be used as the repayment routing channel — incoming fee payments from parents with active credit arrangements will be automatically split between the school's fee balance and the lending partner's repayment share."*

Unlike the Layer 1 commitment (a behavioural promise about student treatment), this is a technical and financial commitment. The admin needs to understand what "automatically split" means in practice before they will sign off.

A concrete example alongside the commitment text de-abstracts it: *"Example: The lending partner credits KES 4,500 to your paybill for a student. When that parent later pays KES 2,000 in fees, approximately KES [repayment share] is routed to repay the credit and KES [remainder] is credited to the student's school fee balance."*

> **Friction point — split feels like a school loss**: The admin may read "payment is split" as income the school loses. The correct frame: the credit disbursement already added money to the school's paybill; repayment routing is recovering that credit advance — the school is not short-changed, it is receiving net-neutral income over the repayment window. The product should make this chain of events visible: (1) lending partner disburses → school gets cash, (2) parent fee payments → school returns lender's share. Net result to school: positive.

> **Cross-reference**: Follows the commitment anxiety pattern from `conventions/journey-patterns.md`. Show the commitment prominently before the confirm action; include it in the post-activation confirmation.

**3. Enabling the feature**
Admin clicks **Enable Fee Top-Up Credit**. Unlike Layer 1, there is no configuration step — no templates to create, no approval mode to set. The product should tell the admin exactly what happens next without requiring action:

*"Fee Top-Up Credit is now enabled. Eligible parents will be identified at the start of the next term and offered fee support."*

The admin's residual concern: *"Will parents think the school is pushing them into credit?"* Pre-empt it in the confirmation message: *"Offers go out under your school's sender ID, but eligibility is determined by Zeraki's payment analysis — not by the school. The school does not select or recommend any individual parent."*

### After
Admin closes settings. Their world is now: at the start of next term, some parents will receive a fee support offer. The school will receive credit disbursements (cash to paybill) from the lending partner for accepted arrangements. Repayment routing will happen automatically on future fee payments. The admin will not interact with the feature again unless they disable it or check the dashboard.

### Established Patterns Used
- Commitment anxiety (non-destructive): commitment statement shown prominently before confirm button; included in post-activation confirmation (extended to financial commitment context from J1, 1.0.1)
- System-prompted next step: confirmation message orients the admin to what happens next without requiring action

## J2 — Parent Receives Offer and Accepts via App (Two-Step Consent)

**Actor**: Parent
**Mental state entering**: Surprised and slightly suspicious. The offer arrived as a push notification — the parent did not initiate this. Their first reaction may be: *"Is this spam? Is the school chasing me for fees?"* Trust must be established before the parent can engage with the content of the offer.

### Trigger
Parent receives a push notification after the eligibility assessment batch completes. They may also encounter an in-app banner when they open the Zeraki app and navigate to their child's fee screen.

> **Trust gap — unsolicited financial offer**: A push notification offering "fee support" is novel and potentially alarming. The notification must immediately signal legitimacy via the school name, not a generic platform sender: *"[School name]: [Student]'s fee payment record qualifies them for fee support this term. Tap to see how it works."* Parents trust the school identity — that sender ID is the primary trust signal.

### Journey Steps

**1. Arriving on the offer screen**
Parent taps the notification. Three things need to land in the first five seconds:
- What's being offered: *"KES [amount] in fee support for [Student name]"*
- How it works: *"Credited directly to [School name]. Repaid through your regular fee payments."*
- That no new payment channel or behaviour is required

The fee amount and student name are the two most visually prominent elements. Explanatory text supports them — it does not lead.

> **Friction point — "fee support" to repayment framing**: Entry language uses "fee support" intentionally (BR-14 — no "loan" language before consent). But a parent who thinks this is a grant will feel misled when they reach the consent step and learn about repayment. The offer screen must include the repayment framing — *"Repaid through your regular fee payments"* — before Consent Step 1. This is progressive disclosure, not deception: the parent sees that something is owed before they encounter the legal consent.

**2. Understanding the mechanics**
Offer screen shows:
- Credit amount available
- How it works: credited to school, repaid through future fee payments
- Estimated repayment schedule (weekly amounts)
- Total repayment amount: credit amount + flat fee, displayed as a KES total — e.g., *"Total to repay: KES [credit + fee]"* (BR-17: KES total, not a percentage)

The repayment schedule is the most decision-relevant content on this screen. Parents think *"what do I owe each week"* not *"what is the credit amount."* If the weekly amount is manageable, they proceed. If it looks alarming relative to their payment pattern, they won't.

> **Design consideration — total cost in KES, not %**: BR-17 requires the flat fee to be shown as a total KES amount (*"Total repayment: KES [credit + flat_fee]"*), not as "5% flat." Parents at this income level anchor on absolute KES, not percentages. "KES 225 on top of KES 4,500" is a more comprehensible cost than "5%."

**3. Consent Step 1 — Data sharing and CRB disclosure**
System shows:
- *"To receive this offer, [School name] will share your fee payment history with our credit partner."*
- *"Repayment activity will be reported to credit reference bureaus."*

CTA: **I understand — continue**

This is the highest-stakes moment in J2. CRB reporting sounds threatening to a parent who associates credit bureaus with blacklisting. The product must not soften the disclosure — it is a real commitment with real consequences — but it must frame it accurately:
- CRB reporting is positive if you repay: it builds a credit history
- CRB reporting becomes negative only if repayments are missed
- The data shared is fee payment history at this school, not general financial data

> **Friction point — CRB anxiety**: A parent already stressed about fees will read "reported to credit reference bureaus" as a threat. High abandonment at this step is a signal the framing is wrong — not that the product has an inherent problem. Consider whether a "What does this mean?" expandable explanation is appropriate here, or whether the consent text should proactively reframe: *"Repaying on time builds your credit history. CRB reporting only has negative consequences if repayments are missed."*

> **Compliance note**: The consent text must be reviewed against CBK Digital Credit Provider Regulations 2022 and Kenya Data Protection Act 2019 before launch. Consent version must be stored on the arrangement record (BR-09). *(See OQ-13.)*

**4. Consent Step 2 — Final confirmation**
System shows an explicit, specific confirmation:
*"Confirm fee top-up of KES [amount] for [Student name]. Repayment of approximately KES [amount] per week starts from your next fee payment."*

CTA: **Confirm**

This is the point of no return. The **Confirm** button must not be reachable before the content is fully loaded. No dark patterns: back/dismiss must be clearly available at all steps. The validity of consent depends on it being freely given.

**5. Post-acceptance confirmation**
In-app confirmation: *"Your request is confirmed. We're processing the fee credit to [School name] — you'll receive an SMS once it's been credited."*

This is accurate: ACCEPTED status triggers disbursement, but disbursement has processing latency. The confirmation should not say "credited to school" until it is — an in-progress state on the fee screen (*"Fee top-up processing…"*) bridges the gap until the DISBURSEMENT confirmation webhook fires. Without this, the parent checks the school balance, sees no change, and calls the school.

> **Critical follow-through — waterfall expectation**: This is the best moment to set the waterfall repayment expectation. A callout immediately post-acceptance — *"When you make your next fee payment to [paybill], part will go toward repaying your fee top-up and the rest will go to your school balance. You'll receive an SMS after each payment showing the split."* — eliminates the confusion that will otherwise occur at J6.

### After
Parent returns to the fee screen showing "Fee top-up processing." When disbursement is confirmed, the screen updates to show the credited amount. Their next fee payment will be split — but they've been told what to expect. The in-app experience is the best place to surface the waterfall mechanic before it happens.

### Established Patterns Used
- System-prompted (unexpected offer): unsolicited offer; trust signal (school sender ID) established before content
- Commitment anxiety (non-destructive): disclosure shown before confirm button; consent version tracked (BR-09)
- Two-step consent (disclosure + confirmation): disclosure of data sharing and CRB at Step 1; amount confirmation at Step 2 — new pattern, see `conventions/journey-patterns.md`

## J3 — Parent Receives and Accepts Offer via SMS (Two-Step Consent)

**Actor**: Parent
**Mental state entering**: More guarded than the app path. The parent received an unsolicited SMS from the school's sender ID — a channel that carries both trust (it's the school) and vulnerability (SMS financial scams are common). The keyword-reply UX demands literacy and attention with no visual design to aid comprehension.

### Trigger
Parent receives the credit offer SMS: *"[School]: Based on [Student]'s payment record, you may qualify for fee support this term. Reply FEES to see your offer, or ignore to skip."*

> **Trust gap (SMS)**: SMS offers for financial products are a known fraud vector. The school sender ID is the primary trust signal — parents recognise their school's name. "You may qualify" (not "you are approved") is the correct expectation-setting tone.

### Journey Steps

**1. Reading the offer SMS**
Parent reads the message. They check the sender ID. The word "qualify" signals this is conditional — they will need to do something to see the offer. Three options: reply FEES, ignore, or reply NO.

**2. Replying FEES**
Parent sends FEES. The 30-minute consent session opens.

> **Keyword decision — FEES over TOPUP**: "TOPUP" was the keyword in the original spec. It is changed here because in Kenya, "top-up" is strongly associated with airtime and mobile data purchases — a parent who reads TOPUP may believe they are initiating a telco transaction and end up inside a credit consent flow without that intent. That is a compliance problem, not just a UX friction point. FEES is unambiguous: it cannot be confused with a telco command, it directly names the product domain, and it is short enough to type easily. SUPPORT is an acceptable alternative if FEES tests poorly. TOPUP must not be the keyword for this flow.

**3. Consent Step 1 SMS**
System replies: *"[School] fee top-up: [Student]'s payment history will be shared with our credit partner. Repayments reported to credit bureaus. Reply YES to continue or NO to cancel."*

This is 160-character compressed consent. A parent on a feature phone reading a small screen has limited visual bandwidth. The CRB implication is present but may not fully register.

> **Friction point — consent comprehension via SMS**: The SMS consent format may be functionally compliant but cognitively demanding. A parent who replies YES without fully processing the CRB implication may later contest the arrangement. The product team and legal should confirm whether SMS consent meets the regulatory standard for informed consent under CBK Digital Credit Provider Regulations. *(OQ-13)*

> **Cross-reference**: Inbound SMS keyword pattern (J3, J10 — 1.0.1). Multi-step SMS flow with session timeout.

**4. Parent replies YES**
Session continues.

**5. Consent Step 2 SMS**
System replies: *"Confirm: KES [amount] fee top-up for [Student]. ~KES [amount]/[week or month] from your future fee payments. Reply CONFIRM to accept."*

> **Design consideration — "~" (approximately)**: The tilde signals approximation, but doesn't explain why. A brief note — "(exact amount depends on each payment)" — would clarify, but may push the message over 160 characters. The tradeoff is between character economy and comprehension.

**6. Parent replies CONFIRM**
Consent recorded. Arrangement moves to ACCEPTED. Disbursement triggered.

**7. Acceptance confirmation SMS**
*"[School]: KES [amount] fee top-up confirmed for [Student] and credited to the school. Repayment starts from your next fee payment. Pay to [paybill] as usual."*

*"Pay to [paybill] as usual"* is the most important phrase in this message. It pre-empts the question every parent will have: *"Do I need to change how I pay?"* Answer: No.

> **Waterfall expectation (SMS path)**: Unlike the app path, there is no in-app state to show post-acceptance. The acceptance SMS must do the work: the phrasing *"Repayment starts from your next fee payment"* sets the expectation that the next payment will be split. This is correct but light — parents who need more detail will not have it until the first waterfall SMS arrives (J6).

### After
Parent's next fee payment will be split. The SMS path has no persistent in-app state to reinforce this expectation — the acceptance SMS is the only preparation. Minimising J6 surprise depends on this message being clear.

### Established Patterns Used
- Inbound SMS keyword pattern: multi-step SMS flow initiated by keyword, with session timeout and re-entry path (J3, J10 — 1.0.1)
- Silent session expiry (SMS): 30-minute window; parent can re-initiate by sending FEES again (J3 — 1.0.1)
- Two-step consent (disclosure + confirmation): adapted for SMS compression constraints

## J4 — Parent Declines the Offer

**Actor**: Parent
**Mental state entering**: Decided. The parent has seen the offer (via app or SMS) and has chosen not to accept. Reasons vary: repayment feels risky, CRB disclosure alarmed them, they have other arrangements, or they don't trust the product. The product's job is to close gracefully without damaging trust.

### Trigger
- **App**: Parent taps **No thanks** at any point before final confirmation
- **SMS**: Parent replies NO or STOP to the offer SMS or Step 1 disclosure

### Journey Steps

**1. Declining via App**
Parent taps **No thanks**. Arrangement transitions to DECLINED. No SMS is sent — the parent made an active choice and no confirmation is necessary (correct per spec). The offer disappears from the app without friction.

The product must not use guilt UI — no "Are you sure? You might miss out." patterns. These are harmful in a credit product where the parent's autonomy to decline must be respected and designed clearly. The CTA label "No thanks" is appropriately light; it does not label the parent's decision as an error.

**2. Declining via SMS**
Parent replies NO. Session closes. Arrangement transitions to DECLINED. System does not reply — correct per spec. A parent who replied NO does not want further communication about this offer.

> **Hard requirement — STOP must be session-scoped**: STOP sent during a credit consent SMS session must terminate only that consent session. It must not trigger a global SMS opt-out from the school's sender ID. A parent who replies STOP to decline a credit offer must continue to receive Layer 1 instalment reminders, fee notifications, and all other school SMS communications. A global opt-out would silently break those flows — the parent would stop receiving reminders they depend on without knowing why. This is a non-negotiable implementation constraint, not a "confirm with the product team" question. The technical solution (session-scoped keyword interception before the carrier opt-out layer) must be confirmed with the SMS provider before this feature ships. Carried into the feature spec as BR-20.

**3. After declining**
The arrangement is DECLINED. The parent will not receive a re-offer in the same term. Their eligibility record is preserved for next term's assessment.

> **Gap — no re-offer path within term**: A parent who declines and later changes their mind (e.g., after an unexpected expense) has no recourse within Zeraki. The bursar cannot re-trigger an offer. This is consistent with the spec, but generates inbound calls when declined parents subsequently struggle. Consider whether a grace period before DECLINED is finalised (e.g., 24 hours to reverse) is worth adding — flag as a product decision.

### After
Parent's world is unchanged. No credit arrangement exists. The bursar has no visibility into individual decline reasons — they see an aggregate declined count on the dashboard. The lending partner is not notified of individual declines.

## J5 — Offer Expires Without Response

**Actor**: Parent (passive)
**Mental state entering**: Unaware. The parent has not engaged with the offer — either they missed the notification, ignored the SMS, or intended to respond but didn't.

### Trigger
The offer expiry date is reached with no response (neither acceptance nor decline).

### Journey Steps

**1. Expiry event fires**
System transitions arrangement to EXPIRED. System sends expiry SMS: *"[School]: Your fee top-up offer for [Student] has expired. Contact the school if you still need support."*

**2. Parent receives expiry SMS**
The parent now knows they missed an offer. This creates two outcomes: frustration/regret (especially if they are still struggling with fees) and likely an inbound call to the school.

> **Friction point — expiry SMS generates unnecessary inbound calls**: Every parent who missed the offer and is still fee-constrained will call the bursar. But the bursar cannot reinstate the offer — the dashboard is read-only and the arrangement is EXPIRED. The call is a dead end. The expiry SMS should channel the parent's expectation forward, not backward: *"[School]: Your fee top-up offer for [Student] has expired. Eligible parents will be reassessed at the start of next term."* This answers the parent's implicit question (*"Can I still get this?"*) without generating a call the bursar cannot resolve.

### After
Parent remains with their original fee balance. No credit arrangement exists. They may be re-assessed and re-offered at the start of next term if still eligible.

## J6 — Parent Makes a Fee Payment — Waterfall Allocation Runs

**Actor**: Parent (payment trigger) + System (execution)
**Mental state entering**: Routine. The parent is paying fees as normal — same paybill, same process. They are not thinking about their credit arrangement.

> **Core UX insight**: This is the moment the credit arrangement becomes viscerally real. The parent sends KES 2,000 to the school and receives an SMS saying only KES 1,400 was credited to the school balance. Without prior preparation, this feels like a shortfall — or worse, like the system made an error.

### Trigger
Parent makes a fee payment to the school's M-Pesa paybill. Paybill webhook fires. System detects an ACTIVE_REPAYMENT arrangement for this student.

### Journey Steps

**1. Payment received — no parent friction**
Parent pays as normal. Nothing has changed about the payment channel or process.

**2. Waterfall SMS arrives**
Parent receives: *"[School]: KES [amount] received for [Student]. KES [repayment] applied to fee top-up. Remaining: KES [balance]. School fees: KES [fee_credit] credited."*

This message contains three numbers the parent must process simultaneously: payment received, repayment applied to credit, school fee credited, and remaining credit balance. Cognitive load is high.

> **Friction point — split feels like a loss**: A parent expecting the full payment to go to school fees will read this as *"I only paid KES [fee_credit] off my school fees."* This is accurate but incomplete. The parent reduced their credit balance AND their school fee obligation with the same payment. The SMS must make both reductions visible as progress: *"KES [repayment] off your fee top-up balance. KES [fee_credit] off your school fees."* The framing is "total debt reduced" — not "payment was taken away."

> **Preparation is the primary mitigation**: The best UX solution is not a better-worded SMS — it is the expectation set at J2 / J3 acceptance. A parent who was told at acceptance that *"part of each payment will go to repayment and the rest to school fees"* reads the waterfall SMS as confirmation. A parent who was not told reads it as a surprise. The acceptance journey (J2, J3) is the leverage point; J6 is where the gap surfaces.

**3. Edge — payment less than scheduled instalment**
Parent sends less than the weekly repayment amount. Entire payment goes to repayment share. School fees credited zero.

SMS (per spec): *"KES [amount] received for [Student]. Full amount applied to your fee top-up balance. Remaining: KES [balance]. School balance unchanged this payment."*

> **Friction point — school balance unchanged**: This is the most alarming possible outcome. The parent paid fees and the school balance did not move. The reason — credit repayment takes full priority — is explained in the SMS, but the framing matters. The SMS should lead with what *did* reduce: *"KES [amount] applied to fee top-up. Your combined balance (school fees + top-up) reduced by KES [amount]."* The parent needs to see that they are making progress even when the school balance is unchanged. *(See OQ-09 — confirm priority order with lending partner.)*

### After
Parent's credit balance is lower. Their school fee balance is lower (unless the small-payment edge case applies). The pattern repeats automatically with each payment until the credit balance reaches zero. The parent is making no new decisions.

### Established Patterns Used
- Active record + passive oversight: waterfall allocation runs automatically; SMS serves as the allocation receipt
- SMS as receipt: each waterfall event generates a ledger-style SMS confirmation (J7 pattern, 1.0.1)

## J7 — Credit Arrangement Completes

**Actor**: Parent + System
**Mental state entering**: Unaware — the completion happens automatically with a regular fee payment. The parent may not have been tracking their remaining credit balance.

### Trigger
Waterfall allocation reduces the remaining credit balance to zero. Triggered by a fee payment (same paybill webhook as J6).

### Journey Steps

**1. Completion fires**
System transitions arrangement to COMPLETED. Sends completion SMS: *"[School]: Your fee top-up for [Student] is fully repaid. Thank you for keeping your commitment."*

**2. Parent receives the SMS**
This is a positive closing moment. "Thank you for keeping your commitment" is warm and appropriate — it acknowledges a personal commitment kept, not just a transactional closure.

> **Design consideration — remaining school balance**: The parent may now wonder: *"What's my school fee balance?"* The completion SMS can close the loop: *"[School]: Your fee top-up for [Student] is fully repaid. Remaining school balance: KES [balance]. Thank you for keeping your commitment."* This saves the parent a separate inquiry to the school.

**3. Bursar dashboard updates**
Arrangement moves from active count to completed count. No action required from the bursar.

### After
Parent's credit arrangement is closed. Their next fee payment goes entirely to school fees — no more split. They may be re-assessed next term for a new offer if still eligible. The completion data (repayment history) feeds positively into the next term's credit score (improving the payment consistency and plan fulfilment components).

## J8 — Waterfall Breaks — Child Withdrawn

**Actor**: System + Lending Partner
**Human actor visibility**: Limited. Neither the parent nor the bursar is actively present at this transition.

> **Note**: This is primarily a system-to-system event. The UX impact on human actors is downstream — specifically what the parent and bursar experience *after* the system detects the withdrawal and flags the arrangement AT_RISK.

### Trigger
System detects that a student with an ACTIVE_REPAYMENT arrangement has been withdrawn or transferred. Detection mechanism is unresolved — see OQ-10.

### Journey Steps

**1. AT_RISK transition**
System flags the arrangement AT_RISK. Notifies the lending partner.

**2. Bridging SMS to parent** *(recommended addition to feature spec)*
System sends a bridging SMS to the parent: *"[School]: Your fee top-up balance for [Student] remains outstanding. The credit partner may contact you about the remaining balance."*

> **Requirement — bridging SMS on AT_RISK transition**: The current feature spec includes no parent notification at this stage. Under that spec, the first post-withdrawal communication the parent receives is a demand letter or CRB listing notice from the lending partner — with no prior word from Zeraki. This is a disproportionate trust harm: the product presented itself as supportive and low-friction, and the first adverse-outcome communication arrives from an unfamiliar third party without context. A single SMS at the AT_RISK transition bridges this gap. It is not debt collection — Zeraki is not chasing the parent. It is a factual notification consistent with the existing SMS contract pattern for all other state transitions in this feature. This recommendation is carried into the feature spec as a new step in Flow 10 and a new SMS Contracts entry.

**3. Lending partner resolution**
Lending partner determines the recovery path (extended collection, CRB listing, or write-off). Zeraki takes no further action.

### After
Zeraki's involvement ends. The arrangement may eventually close as COMPLETED (if repaid) or DEFAULTED (if written off). The school's cash flow is unaffected — the credit was disbursed and the school received its share of earlier fee payments.

## J9 — Bursar Views the Credit Dashboard

**Actor**: Bursar
**Mental state entering**: Checking in. The bursar navigates to the credit section either as a routine Finance review or after receiving a dashboard alert that a new disbursement has arrived. They are not making decisions — they are assessing the school's credit-related cash position and handling potential parent enquiries.

### Trigger
- **Routine**: Bursar navigates to Fee Top-Up Credit during their regular Finance review
- **Notification-prompted**: Bursar sees a dashboard indicator that a new credit disbursement has been credited to the school's paybill

### Journey Steps

**1. Arriving on the term summary**
Bursar sees:
- **Total disbursed to school this term** — the cash the school has already received from credit arrangements
- Active arrangements count
- Completed arrangements count
- At-risk arrangements count (if any)

Total disbursed is the primary value metric — it answers the bursar's first question: *"How much has the school benefited from this feature?"*

**2. Per-student list**
Each student: credit amount, disbursement date, repayment collected to date, remaining balance, status.

> **Friction point — bursar as parent support channel**: Parents will call the school about their credit arrangements. The bursar needs enough detail to answer basic questions — *"Has my fee top-up been credited?", "How much have I repaid?", "What's my remaining balance?"* — without escalating to Zeraki support. The individual arrangement detail view (disbursement timeline + waterfall allocations) must be accessible and clear from this screen.

**3. Read-only constraint**
Bursar cannot approve, modify, or cancel arrangements. They are observers only.

> **Friction point — bursar helplessness on escalations**: A parent frustrated about a disbursement failure or unexpected waterfall split will direct their frustration at the bursar. The bursar can see the status but has no action to take. The dashboard should provide a clear escalation route — *"For issues with a specific arrangement, contact Zeraki Finance support: [contact]"* — so the bursar can route the parent's enquiry correctly rather than being a dead end.

**4. At-risk count**
An at-risk count signals a potential problem. The bursar may interpret AT_RISK as requiring school action. The dashboard must clarify immediately: *"The lending partner has been notified and is managing recovery. No action is needed from the school."* Without this, the bursar may spend time trying to contact parents about an issue outside their scope.

**5. Proactive notification consideration**
The spec does not require a notification to the bursar when a disbursement occurs. But since the bursar is the first contact point for parent enquiries, a dashboard badge or notification on any new disbursement or DISBURSEMENT_FAILED event would allow the bursar to proactively manage conversations. Flag as a design consideration.

### After
Bursar closes the dashboard with a clear picture of the school's credit cash position. They can handle basic parent enquiries from the detail view. Their role is to observe and relay — not to manage credit arrangements directly.

### Established Patterns Used
- Active record + passive oversight: dashboard updates automatically as payments and disbursements arrive
- Destructive action on active record: not applicable (read-only dashboard) — but the absence of actions must be communicated to prevent confusion

## J10 — Disbursement Fails

**Actor**: Parent + Bursar
**Mental state entering**: Parent — expectant. They confirmed a credit arrangement and are waiting for confirmation that the school has been credited. Bursar — unaware until they check the dashboard.

> **Note**: This is the error path in Flow 7. The parent has given valid consent and accepted the arrangement — they expect the credit to flow. A disbursement failure is a trust breach: not because Zeraki did anything wrong, but because the gap between ACCEPTED and DISBURSED was visible and then broken.

### Trigger
- **Lending partner rejection**: API returns a rejection response after the parent accepts
- **Timeout**: Lending partner API is unreachable after maximum retries (see OQ-07)

### Journey Steps

**1. Arrangement transitions to DISBURSEMENT_FAILED**
System marks the arrangement failed. Parent receives SMS: *"[School]: We could not process your fee top-up at this time. Please contact the school for assistance."*

**2. Parent receives the failure SMS**
The parent accepted an offer and was told it was confirmed. Now they receive a failure. The failure SMS routes them to "the school" — but:
- The bursar may not yet know about the failure
- The bursar has no resolution path (read-only dashboard)
- The parent doesn't know why it failed

> **Friction point — "contact the school" without context or resolution path**: The failure SMS should route the issue correctly: *"[School]: We were unable to process your fee top-up at this time. Please contact Zeraki Finance support at [contact] or ask your school's bursar to flag this to Zeraki."* This makes clear who owns resolution (Zeraki, not the bursar) while preserving the school as a relay.

**3. Bursar becomes aware (when they check the dashboard)**
Bursar sees DISBURSEMENT_FAILED on the arrangement. They have no action available. The dashboard should provide a direct escalation path — *"Arrangement failed. Contact Zeraki Finance support to investigate: [contact]"* — so the bursar can resolve the parent conversation rather than deflecting.

> **Design consideration — proactive bursar notification on failure**: The spec does not require a notification to the bursar on disbursement failure. If the parent calls the school before the bursar has checked the dashboard, the bursar has no information. A notification (dashboard badge or email) on any DISBURSEMENT_FAILED event would let the bursar get ahead of the parent conversation.

### After
The parent's arrangement is DISBURSEMENT_FAILED. Their eligibility record is unchanged — they remain eligible. But there is no automatic retry. Manual investigation is required (OQ-06). This is the worst UX outcome in the product: the parent raised their hand, gave consent, and was stranded. Reducing DISBURSEMENT_FAILED frequency via API reliability and retry policy (OQ-07) is the primary mitigation.

## Cross-Journey Notes

### Language discipline: "loan" prohibition
The "loan" prohibition (BR-14, Global Business Rules) applies across all parent-facing surfaces. Mapping across journeys:

| Journey moment | Required language |
|---------------|------------------|
| Offer notification and offer screen (J2, J3) | "fee support", "fee top-up" |
| Consent Step 1 (J2, J3) | "credit partner" — may reference credit nature but not "loan" |
| Consent Step 2 (J2, J3) | "fee top-up" for product; KES amounts for repayment |
| Post-acceptance and waterfall SMS (J6, J7) | "fee top-up balance", "repayment" |
| Completion (J7) | "fee top-up", "commitment" — positive framing |

### The waterfall expectation gap
The most structurally important UX intervention in this feature is setting the waterfall repayment expectation at acceptance — before the first payment is split. Every parent who accepts (J2, J3) must understand the split *before* it happens. Every instance of J6 confusion traces back to whether J2/J3 did this work. The in-app "How repayment works" callout at acceptance (J2) and the phrasing *"Repayment starts from your next fee payment"* in the acceptance SMS (J3) are the two leverage points that determine whether J6 is a confirmation or a surprise.

### The bursar's role gap
The bursar's read-only constraint (BR-13) creates a gap between the trust parents have in the bursar and the capabilities the product gives them. Every journey that ends in "contact the school" (J5, J10) routes through the bursar. The product must either:
1. Give the bursar enough information and escalation paths to handle enquiries (full arrangement detail + direct Zeraki support route)
2. Or provide a direct parent-facing support contact that does not require the bursar as an intermediary

This is not resolved in the current feature spec and should be raised as a design consideration before the product ships.

### Trust at every handoff
This feature introduces three trust handoffs that are each potential breakpoints: (1) Zeraki → Parent at offer delivery (trust that the offer is legitimate); (2) Zeraki → Lending Partner at disbursement (trust that the credit will flow); (3) Lending Partner → Parent at AT_RISK events (trust in the product's integrity even in adverse outcomes). Each handoff requires deliberate design — not just a functional state transition.
